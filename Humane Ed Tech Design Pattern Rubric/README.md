## 🎯 Purpose

Educational technology is shaping how millions of students learn — yet most tools are evaluated primarily on engagement metrics, not on whether they support genuine learning, autonomy, and long-term well-being.

This rubric fills that gap. It provides a structured, research-backed framework to assess ed tech tools on **five humane design dimensions**, helping:

- **Students & parents** make wiser choices about which tools to use
- **Teachers** evaluate tools before recommending them to learners
- **Ed tech designers & developers** audit and improve their own products

> The goal is not to cast judgment, but to surface tradeoffs — empowering users and builders to make more intentional choices.

---

## 📐 The Rubric

The rubric evaluates tools across **five dimensions**, each grounded in peer-reviewed research:

| Dimension | Guiding Question |
|-----------|-----------------|
| 🧠 **Cognitive Design** | Does the tool support how humans actually learn, remember, and build understanding? |
| 💬 **Emotional & Motivational Design** | Does the tool promote intrinsic motivation, curiosity, and emotional safety? |
| 🎯 **Attention & Focus Ecology** | Does the tool respect users' attention and promote deep engagement rather than distraction? |
| 🕹 **Agency & Autonomy** | Does the tool empower learners with control, choice, and understanding of how it works? |
| 🤝 **Social & Ethical Design** | Does the tool foster respectful collaboration, inclusion, and ethical technology practices? |

### Scoring Key

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **Strongly Humane** — Actively supports human learning, autonomy, and well-being; evidence-based. |
| ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **Generally Humane** — Positive alignment with humane design; some room for improvement. |
| ⭐⭐⭐ | **Mixed / Context-Dependent** — Has benefits but also potential tradeoffs. |
| ⭐⭐ | **Needs Caution** — May cause harm or distraction if not managed carefully. |
| ⭐ | **Problematic** — Conflicts with humane or human-centered learning principles. |

Each design pattern is evaluated with:
- A **description** of what the pattern does
- A **research basis** (e.g., Dweck, 2006; Roediger & Karpicke, 2006)
- A **humane design score** (1–5)
- **Tradeoffs & notes** for nuanced context

## 🧪 Example Evaluation: Khan Academy

To demonstrate the rubric in action, we applied it to **Khan Academy** as a worked example.

### Summary of Scores

| Dimension | Score | Key Insight |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| 🧠 Cognitive Design | **4.7 / 5** | Mastery learning and retrieval practice are core strengths |
| 💬 Emotional & Motivational | **4.0 / 5** | Balanced; could deepen reflection and metacognitive prompts |
| 🎯 Attention & Focus | **4.3 / 5** | Calm, focused interface; gamification is moderate |
| 🕹 Agency & Autonomy | **4.3 / 5** | Excellent learner control; AI transparency could improve |
| 🤝 Social & Ethical | **4.0 / 5** | Inclusive ethos; community Q&A quality is uneven |

**💡 Overall Humane Design Rating: 4.3 / 5 — "Strongly Humane"**

#### Strengths
- Deep grounding in learning science and mastery pedagogy
- Intrinsic motivation focus — minimal harmful competition
- Expanding access and inclusion globally
- Calm, focus-friendly learning environment

#### Areas for Improvement
- Make AI adaptivity and logic more transparent to learners and teachers
- Add guided reflection prompts to consolidate learning
- Improve moderation and curation of community Q&A

## 📋 How to Use This Rubric

1. **Download** the rubric template (see `/rubric/template.md`)
2. **Choose** an ed tech tool to evaluate
3. **Score** each design pattern collaboratively — with students, teachers, or your team
4. **Support each rating** with observed examples (screenshots, usage notes)
5. **Record tradeoffs** — humane design often involves balancing engagement with autonomy
6. **Share your evaluation** to help build a community database of tool assessments

> 💡 Works best as a **collaborative activity** — diverse perspectives surface tradeoffs that individual evaluators miss.

## 📚 Research Foundation

This rubric draws on a curated set of foundational learning science and humane tech research:

- Bloom (1984) — Mastery learning
- Cepeda et al. (2006); Kang (2016) — Spaced repetition
- Roediger & Karpicke (2006) — Retrieval practice / testing effect
- Vygotsky (1978); Sweller (1988) — Scaffolding and cognitive load
- Mayer (2009) — Multimedia learning
- Dweck (2006) — Growth mindset and mastery motivation
- Hattie & Timperley (2007) — Feedback and learning
- Deci & Ryan (1985) — Self-determination theory
- Zimmerman (2002) — Self-regulated learning
- Csikszentmihalyi (1990) — Flow and attention
- Fogg (2009); Alter (2017) — Persuasive design and habit loops
- Locke & Latham (2002) — Goal-setting theory
- Shneiderman (2020) — Human-centered AI
- Norman (2013) — User-centered design
- Chi (2009) — Interactive learning
- Walton & Cohen (2007) — Belonging and inclusion
- UNESCO (2023) — Inclusive education
- OECD (2021) — Data privacy and ethics
